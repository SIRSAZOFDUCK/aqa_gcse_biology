What organisation of an ecosystem covers

Every subtopic below has its own questions, mark schemes and notes.

4.7.2.1Food chains, predators and sampling

Every food chain starts with a producer that builds biomass using energy from light. This sub-point also carries the fieldwork: quadrats, transects and the maths for turning a handful of counts into an estimate for a whole habitat.

4.7.2.2Cycling materials — water and carbon

There is no new supply of materials on Earth, so everything living things are built from has to be released and used again. Two cycles are examinable — carbon and water — and microorganisms drive both by breaking down what is left behind.

4.7.2.3Decomposition, compost and biogas

Biology-only content. Decay is enzyme-controlled, so anything that affects the decomposers' enzymes affects the rate: temperature, water and oxygen. Required practical 10 measures that rate using lipase, milk and a colour change.

4.7.2.4Impact of environmental change

Biology-only and Higher Tier only. Three environmental changes shift where species can live — temperature, water availability and the composition of atmospheric gases — and the questions usually ask you to weigh up evidence rather than recall a fact.

Key terms

The definitions examiners expect, in the wording that earns the mark.

Producer — An organism that makes its own food molecules using energy transferred from light by photosynthesis.
Biomass — The mass of living material in an organism.
Consumer — An organism that gets its food by eating other organisms.
Predator — A consumer that hunts and kills other animals for food.
Prey — An animal that is hunted and eaten by a predator.
Distribution — Where an organism is found within a habitat.
Abundance — How many individuals of a species there are in an area.
Quadrat — A square frame enclosing a known area, used for sampling.
Transect — A line across a habitat along which organisms are recorded, used to study how distribution changes.
Percentage cover — The percentage of a quadrat's area that is covered by a particular species.
Abiotic — The non-living parts of an ecosystem, such as air, water and soil.
Biotic — The living parts of an ecosystem.
Decay — The breakdown of dead material and waste by microorganisms.
Detritus feeder — An animal that feeds on dead remains and waste material.
Mineral ions — Ions released into the soil by decay and absorbed by plant roots for growth.
Combustion — Burning — it releases carbon dioxide from wood and fossil fuels.
Transpiration — The loss of water vapour from a plant.
Condensation — Water vapour cooling and turning back into liquid water.
Precipitation — Water falling from clouds as rain, snow or hail.
Decomposer — A microorganism, such as a bacterium or fungus, that breaks down dead material and waste.
Denatured — An enzyme whose active site has changed shape, so it no longer works.
Optimum temperature — The temperature at which the rate of a process is fastest.
Compost — Decomposed organic matter used as a natural fertiliser.
Anaerobic decay — Decay by microorganisms in the absence of oxygen; it produces methane.
Biogas — A fuel gas, mainly methane, produced by the anaerobic decay of waste material.
Lipase — The enzyme used in the required practical; it breaks fats down into fatty acids.
Phenolphthalein — An indicator that is pink in alkaline conditions and colourless below about pH 8.3.
Seasonal change — An environmental change that repeats with the time of year.
Geographic change — A change caused by the physical features of an area, such as altitude or soil.
Indicator species — A species whose presence or absence shows something about environmental conditions, such as lichen and air pollution.
Global warming — The rise in average global temperature, caused by human activity increasing greenhouse gases.

Where marks get lost

The mistakes that come up again and again in this topic.

  • Draw food chain arrows from the eaten to the eater — they follow the food, not the hunter.
  • Count consumers, not boxes: in a four-organism chain the last organism is the tertiary consumer, not the fourth consumer of the chain including the plant.
  • On a predator-prey graph, identify the predator by which curve peaks second, not by which peaks are smaller.
  • When the quadrat is not 1 m², convert the mean to a number per m² before multiplying by the area — and remember a smaller quadrat means a bigger number per square metre.
  • A transect is deliberately not random: random sampling is for estimating abundance across a whole area, while a transect is for showing change along a line.
  • Decay does not release carbon dioxide by itself — it is the respiration of the decomposers that does. Say so.
  • Plants respire all the time, not only in the dark, so they release carbon dioxide as well as absorbing it.
  • Carbon enters an animal in its food, not through its lungs; breathing in supplies oxygen.
  • The water cycle provides fresh water — the word fresh is often the mark.
  • The nitrogen cycle is not on this specification, so do not spend revision time on it.
  • Enzymes are denatured, not killed — organisms are what get killed. Using the wrong word loses the mark.
  • "Warmer is always faster" is only true up to the optimum; past it the rate falls.
  • Rate = 1000 ÷ time, so a longer time means a slower rate; check your answer is the right way round.
  • Give the unit s⁻¹ when calculating rate, and watch for questions asking for three significant figures.
  • Naming a factor is only half of a "describe and explain" answer — say how the factor affects the decomposers or their enzymes.
  • This sub-point is Higher Tier only, and biology only — it is not on the combined science course.
  • "Evaluate" means give both sides and then a judgement; answers that only agree, or only criticise, rarely reach full marks.
  • Not every change is caused by humans — seasonal and geographic causes are named in the specification too.
  • Say which of the three changes you are using (temperature, water, atmospheric gases) rather than writing vaguely about "the environment".
